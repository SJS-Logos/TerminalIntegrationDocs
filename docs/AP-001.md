# 1. Introduction

This RFC defines the architectural principles governing the structure of all software services within this ecosystem.

The objective is to establish a technology-independent architecture that promotes maintainability, testability, portability, and long-term consistency.

This RFC defines architectural invariants only. It does not prescribe implementation technologies, deployment models, or programming languages.

*Note on Legacy Terminology:* This document defines **logical boundaries**. It replaces prior internal standards that defined physical folder names (such as `Api`, `Core`, `InfrastructureAbstractions`, `Infrastructure`, or `Worker`). The principles defined here govern how responsibilities are separated, not necessarily how directories are named in source control.

Detailed structural requirements are specified in subsequent RFCs.

# 3. Terminology

This section defines the terms used throughout this RFC.

**Service**: An independently deployable unit of software composed of an Application, a Domain, and the Domain's Capabilities.

**Application**: The architectural area that orchestrates use cases, defines inputs and outputs (Contract Models), and manages transaction and security boundaries. It acts as a structural facade over the Domain. How use cases coordinate operations within the Domain is deferred to a later RFC.

**Domain**: The architectural area that expresses pure business meaning, state, and rules.

**Capability**: A domain-owned abstraction describing something the Domain requires, expressed in domain terms and independent of any technology.

**Core**: The Application, the Domain, and their Capabilities.

**Implementation**: A delivery mechanism interacting with the Application, or a concrete realization of a Capability using a specific technology. Implementations exist outside the architectural model.

**Entity**: A domain object defined by a stable identity that persists across changes to its state [3].

**Value Object**: An immutable domain object defined by its attributes rather than by identity [3].

**Domain Service**: A stateless domain operation expressing business behaviour that does not naturally belong to a single Entity or Value Object [3].

**Contract Model**: A language-neutral structure describing an input, output, or message exchanged with the Application. Contract Models include Request Models, Response Models, and Message Models.

**Business Decision**: A determination of a business outcome governed by business rules (for example, whether a reservation is allowed or a payment is authorised), as distinct from a technical operation.

# 4. Motivation

Modern software frequently couples business logic to transport protocols, infrastructure technologies, and deployment environments.

Such coupling commonly results in:

* Reduced testability.
* Increased deployment complexity.
* Difficult technology migration.
* Large end-to-end test suites.
* Inconsistent service implementations.

This architecture separates business behaviour from implementation concerns, allowing business functionality to be validated independently of deployment.

## 4.1 Logical Architecture vs. Physical Deployment

This architecture defines **logical separation** of concerns, not physical deployment topology.

The architectural benefits of microservices — modularity, independent testability, clear boundaries, technology independence — can be achieved through logical structure **without incurring the operational overhead of distributed deployment**.

As Martin Fowler has observed, microservices impose significant deployment costs: distributed transaction complexity, network latency, operational monitoring overhead, and resource duplication. These costs are justified only when independent scalability or organizational autonomy require physical separation.

This architecture follows the principle: **make it work, make it right, make it fast** (attributed to Kent Beck, often paraphrased from Knuth's "premature optimization" principle). The logical structure defined here provides modularity and testability (make it work, make it right) without mandating distributed deployment (make it fast — only when needed).

A service structured according to these principles:
- MAY be deployed as a single process (monolith)
- MAY be deployed as multiple independent processes (microservices)
- MAY share resources (database, message bus) with other services
- MAY use dedicated resources for independent scaling

**Deployment topology is an optimization decision**, not an architectural requirement. The architecture enables physical separation when justified by operational needs, but does not impose it prematurely.

The logical boundaries defined in this RFC — Application, Domain, Capabilities, and Implementations — remain invariant regardless of deployment model.

# 5. Architectural Model

Every service SHALL be composed of logical areas nested as follows:

```text
Application
Domain
  └── Capabilities   (domain-owned abstractions)
```

Implementations exist outside this architectural model.

The architecture distinguishes between:

* What a service can do (**Application**, defining use cases and Contract Models).
* What a service knows (**Domain**).
* What the Core requires to function (**Capabilities**, owned by the Domain).

The Application, the Domain, and the Capabilities form the core of the service.

This model derives from Clean Architecture [5], [6], Hexagonal (Ports and Adapters) Architecture [7], and the Onion Architecture [8].

How these concerns are implemented is outside the scope of this RFC.

# 6. Architectural Principles

## 6.1 Decision Ownership

Every business decision SHALL be made within exactly one Domain.

This is the primary architectural invariant. A business decision is a determination of a business outcome governed by business rules. Examples include:

* Is a reservation allowed?
* Is a payment authorised?
* Is a user entitled?
* Is a settlement completed?

The following are technical operations, not business decisions, and MUST NOT determine business outcomes:

* Deserialize JSON.
* Execute SQL.
* Publish a message.
* Retry an HTTP request.

A business decision MUST NOT be made in the Application, in a Capability, or in any Implementation.

---

## 6.2 Application

The Application directs the use cases of the service.

The Application SHALL contain:

* Use cases.
* Contract Models representing inputs, outputs, and messages.
* Transaction and security boundaries.

The Application MUST NOT contain business rules or state, delegating these to the Domain.

The Application MAY depend on the Domain and Capabilities.

Whether coordination between Domain operations belongs to the Application, to Domain Services, or elsewhere is not fixed by this RFC and SHALL be specified in a later RFC.

---

## 6.3 Domain

The Domain defines business meaning [3].

The Domain SHALL distinguish business state from business behaviour.

Business State is modelled by:

* Entities.
* Value Objects.

Business Behaviour is modelled by:

* Business rules.
* Policies.
* Domain Services.

The architecture primarily protects business behaviour. Entities, Value Objects, and Domain Services are the tactical patterns of Domain-Driven Design [3], [4].

The Domain MUST NOT depend upon:

* Transport technologies.
* Persistence technologies.
* Messaging technologies.
* Deployment technologies.
* Framework-specific constructs.

The Domain SHALL express business outcomes without knowledge of how those outcomes are communicated externally.

The Application and Domain SHALL express external requirements as Capabilities (Section 6.4).

---

## 6.4 Capabilities

A Capability is an abstract description of something the Domain requires to fulfil its business rules.

Capabilities are owned by the Domain and expressed in domain terms. They describe *what* the Domain requires, never *how* it is provided.

Typical capabilities include:

* Persistence.
* Time.
* Identity.
* External systems.
* Messaging.

A Capability MUST:

* Be expressed in terms of the Domain.
* Remain independent of any technology, protocol, or product.

A Capability MUST NOT:

* Reference transport, persistence, or messaging technologies.
* Depend upon any Implementation.

Concrete technology is supplied exclusively by Implementations (Section 7).

Capabilities correspond to the driven (secondary) ports of Hexagonal Architecture [7] and apply the Dependency Inversion Principle [9].

---

# 7. Implementations

Implementations interact with the Application or realize Capabilities.

Examples include:

* HTTP endpoints.
* Message subscribers.
* Database implementations.
* Message broker integrations.
* Scheduled tasks.
* Command-line interfaces.

Implementations MAY depend upon any architectural component.

Architectural components MUST NOT depend upon implementations.

---

# 8. Dependency Rules

Dependencies SHALL always move toward business meaning.

Business meaning resides in the Domain. The Application, Domain, and their Capabilities form the core. All Implementations depend upon the core; the core depends upon nothing outside itself.

This is the Dependency Rule of Clean Architecture [5] and an application of the Dependency Inversion Principle [9].

The following dependency directions are permitted:

```text
Implementations  ──▶  Application + Domain + Capabilities
                                  (the core)
```

The following dependencies are prohibited:

* Core to Implementations.
* Application or Domain to transport technologies.
* Application or Domain to persistence technologies.
* Capabilities to implementation technologies.

---

# 9. Architectural Smells

The following are common violations of this architecture. Their presence indicates that a principle defined in this RFC has been broken.

* Business decisions made outside the Domain.
* Transport-specific types appearing in the Domain.
* Capability implementations referenced by the Domain.
* Business rules duplicated across multiple implementations.
* Framework types leaking into architectural components.

This section provides practical review guidance; it does not introduce new requirements beyond those already defined.

---

# 10. Evolution

This RFC defines logical responsibilities only. It intentionally does not prescribe how those responsibilities are physically organized.

Subsequent RFCs SHALL define:

* Service structure.
* Folder layout.
* Naming conventions.
* Dependency rules for concrete units.
* Testing strategy.
* Implementation guidance.

Such RFCs SHALL remain consistent with the invariants defined here, foremost the Decision Ownership invariant (Section 6.1).

---

# 11. Testability

Business behaviour SHALL be executable without deployment.

Business verification MUST NOT require:

* Network communication.
* Databases.
* Message brokers.
* External services.
* Containers.
* Cloud infrastructure.

Implementation-specific behaviour SHALL be verified independently from business behaviour.

End-to-end testing SHOULD primarily validate deployment, integration, and configuration.

This testing strategy is consistent with the test pyramid [10].

---

# 12. Design Goals

This architecture is intended to achieve:

* Independent evolution of business logic and technology.
* Consistent service structure.
* High unit and component test coverage.
* Minimal deployment dependencies during development.
* Replaceable implementation technologies.
* Clear ownership of responsibilities.

---

# 13. Compliance

A service complies with this RFC if:

* Every business decision is made within exactly one Domain.
* All business logic resides within the Domain.
* The Application contains no business behaviour.
* Capabilities remain technology-independent abstractions.
* Implementations are isolated from architectural definitions.
* Business functionality can be executed independently of deployment technologies.
* Dependency rules defined in this RFC are satisfied.
